Many who read this review will have lived through the whole golden age of cardiac surgery. I was born in a northern steel town in the U.K. in 1948. My mother’s account of the “blue baby” who died in the maternity cot next to mine was one of my earliest childhood recollections. Then I watched my grandfather suffer multiple heart attacks and die from heart failure at the age of 62. In the 1950s nothing could be done to help him, or so we were told. In 1955 I watched a television program called “Your Life in Their Hands.” It described the efforts of pioneers in Philadelphia to build a heart-lung machine—futuristic technology...
